7.8.1 Derivatives

For a scalar-argument function f:R→Rf:\mathbb{R}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}f:R→R, we define its derivative at a point xxx the quantity:

f′(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+h)−f(x)hf'(x)=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}f′(x)=h→0lim​hf(x+h)−f(x)​

7.8.2 Gradients

We can extend this definition to vector-argument function f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow \mathbb{R}f:Rn→R, by defining the partial derivative of fff w.r.t i:i:i:

∂f∂xi=lim⁡h→0f(x+hei)−f(x)h\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0} \frac{f(\bold{x}+h\bold{e}_i)-f(\bold{x})}{h}∂xi​∂f​=h→0lim​hf(x+hei​)−f(x)​

where ei\bold{e}_iei​ is the iiith unit vector.

The gradient of a function is its vector of partial derivatives:

g=∂f∂x=∇f=(∂f∂x1⋮∂f∂xn)g=\frac{\partial f}{\partial \bold{x}}=\nabla f = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix}g=∂x∂f​=∇f=​∂x1​∂f​⋮∂xn​∂f​​​

Where the operator ∇\nabla∇ maps a function f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow \mathbb{R}f:Rn→R to another function g:Rn→Rn.g:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n.g:Rn→Rn.

The point at which the gradient is evaluated is noted:

g(x∗)≜∂f∂x∣x∗g(\bold{x}^*)\triangleq \frac{\partial f}{\partial \bold{x}}\Bigg |_{\bold{x}^*}g(x∗)≜∂x∂f​​x∗​

7.8.3 Directional derivative

The directional derivative measure how much f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow\mathbb{R}f:Rn→R changes along a direction in space v\bold{v}v:

Dvf(x)=lim⁡h→0f(x+vh)−f(x)hD_\bold{v}f(\bold{x})=\lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{f(\bold{x}+\bold{v}h)-f(\bold{x})}{h}Dv​f(x)=h→0lim​hf(x+vh)−f(x)​

Note that:

Dvf(x)=∇f(x).vD_{\bold{v}}f(\bold{x})=\nabla f(\bold{x}).\bold{v}Dv​f(x)=∇f(x).v

7.8.4 Total derivative

Suppose the function has the form f(t,x(t),y(t))f(t,x(t),y(t))f(t,x(t),y(t)), we define the total derivative w.r.t ttt as:

dfdt=∂f∂t+∂f∂x∂xdt+∂f∂y∂ydt\frac{df}{dt}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{dt}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{dt}dtdf​=∂t∂f​+∂x∂f​dt∂x​+∂y∂f​dt∂y​

Multiplying by dtdtdt, we get the total differential:

df=∂f∂tdt+∂f∂x∂x+∂f∂y∂ydf=\frac{\partial f}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\partial x+\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\partial ydf=∂t∂f​dt+∂x∂f​∂x+∂y∂f​∂y

This represents how much fff changes when we change ttt.

7.8.5 Jacobian

Consider f:Rn→Rm\bold{f}:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^mf:Rn→Rm. The Jacobian of this function is an m×nm \times nm×n matrix of partial derivatives:

Jf(x)=∂f∂x⊤≜(∂f1∂x1…∂f1∂xn⋮⋱⋮∂fm∂x1…∂fm∂xn)=(∇f1(x)⊤⋮∇fm(x)⊤)J_\bold{f}(\bold{x})=\frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial \bold{x}^\top}\triangleq \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}&\dots& \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1}&\dots& \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n} \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix} \nabla f_1(\bold{x})^\top \\ \vdots \\ \nabla f_m(\bold{x})^\top \end{pmatrix}Jf​(x)=∂x⊤∂f​≜​∂x1​∂f1​​⋮∂x1​∂fm​​​…⋱…​∂xn​∂f1​​⋮∂xn​∂fm​​​​=​∇f1​(x)⊤⋮∇fm​(x)⊤​​

7.8.5.1 Vector Product

The Jacobian vector product (JVP) is right multiplying J∈Rm×nJ\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}J∈Rm×n by v∈Rnv\in\mathbb{R^n}v∈Rn:

Jf(x).v=(∇f1(x)⊤.v⋮∇fm(x)⊤.v)J_\bold{f}(\bold{x}).\bold{v}=\begin{pmatrix} \nabla f_1(\bold{x})^\top.\bold{v} \\ \vdots \\ \nabla f_m(\bold{x})^\top.\bold{v} \end{pmatrix}Jf​(x).v=​∇f1​(x)⊤.v⋮∇fm​(x)⊤.v​​

Similarly, the vector Jacobian product (VJP) is left multiplying by u∈Rmu\in\mathbb{R}^mu∈Rm:

u⊤Jf(x)=u⊤(∂f∂x1…∂f∂x1)=(u⊤∂f∂x1…∂f∂x1)\bold{u}^\top J_{\bold{f}}(\bold{x})= \bold{u}^\top\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial x_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial x_1} \end{pmatrix}= \begin{pmatrix}\bold{u}^\top\frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial x_1}&\dots & \frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial x_1} \end{pmatrix}u⊤Jf​(x)=u⊤(∂x1​∂f​​…​∂x1​∂f​​)=(u⊤∂x1​∂f​​…​∂x1​∂f​​)

7.8.5.2 Composition of feature

The Jacobian of the composition of two features h(x)=g(f(x))h(\bold{x})=g(f(\bold{x}))h(x)=g(f(x)) is obtained with the chain rule:

Jh(x)=Jg(f(x))Jf(x)J_h(\bold{x})=J_g(f(\bold{x}))J_f(\bold{x})Jh​(x)=Jg​(f(x))Jf​(x)

Let f:R→R2f:\mathbb{R}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2f:R→R2 and g:R2→R2g:\mathbb{R}^2\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2g:R2→R2, we have:

∂g∂x=∂g∂f∂f∂x=(∂g1∂f1∂g1∂f2∂g2∂f1∂g2∂f2)(∂f1∂x∂f2∂x)\frac{\partial \bold{g}}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial \bold{g}}{\partial \bold{f}}\frac{\partial \bold{f}}{\partial x}= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial f_1} & \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial f_2} \\ \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial f_1} & \frac{\partial g_2}{\partial f_2} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x} \end{pmatrix}∂x∂g​=∂f∂g​∂x∂f​=(∂f1​∂g1​​∂f1​∂g2​​​∂f2​∂g1​​∂f2​∂g2​​​)(∂x∂f1​​∂x∂f2​​​)

7.8.6 Hessian

For f:Rn→Rf:\mathbb{R}^n\rightarrow\mathbb{R}f:Rn→R that is twice differentiable, the Hessian is the n×nn\times nn×n symmetric matrix of second partial derivatives:

Hf=∂2f∂x2=(∂2f∂x12…∂2f∂x1∂xn⋮∂2f∂xn∂x1…∂2f∂xn2)H_{f}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial \bold{x}^2}= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \dots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_n} \\ & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n \partial x_1} & \dots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \end{pmatrix}Hf​=∂x2∂2f​=​∂x12​∂2f​∂xn​∂x1​∂2f​​…⋮…​∂x1​∂xn​∂2f​∂xn2​∂2f​​​

The Hessian is the Jacobian of the gradient.
