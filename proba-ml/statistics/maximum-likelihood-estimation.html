4.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation

4.2 Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)

4.2.1 Definition

Pick the parameter estimation assigning the highest probability to the training data, defined as:

θ^mle≜arg max⁡θp(D∣θ)\begin{equation} \hat{\theta}_{mle} \triangleq \argmax_{\theta}p(\mathcal{D}|\theta) \end{equation}θ^mle​≜θargmax​p(D∣θ)​​

Wit the i.i.d. assumptions this becomes:

p(D∣θ)=∏n=1Np(yn∣xn,θ)\begin{equation} p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)=\prod^N_{n=1}p(\bold{y}_n|\bold{x}_n,\theta) \end{equation}p(D∣θ)=n=1∏N​p(yn​∣xn​,θ)​​

And the Negative Log Likelihood (since most optimization algorithms are designed to minimize cost functions)

NLL(θ)≜−∑n=1Nlog⁡p(yn∣xn,θ)\begin{equation} NLL(\theta)\triangleq -\sum^N_{n=1}\log p(\bold{y}_n|\bold{x}_n,\theta) \end{equation}NLL(θ)≜−n=1∑N​logp(yn​∣xn​,θ)​​

With

θ^mle=arg min⁡θNLL(θ)\hat{\theta}_{mle}= \argmin_{\theta} NLL(\theta)θ^mle​=θargmin​NLL(θ)

4.2.2 Justification for MLE

MLE can be viewed as a point estimation to the Bayesian posterior p(θ∣D)p(\theta|\mathcal{D})p(θ∣D)

θ^map=arg max⁡θlog⁡p(θ∣D)=arg max⁡log⁡p(D∣θ)+log⁡p(θ)\hat{\theta}_{map}= \argmax_{\theta} \log p(\theta|\mathcal{D})=\argmax \log p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)+\log p(\theta)θ^map​=θargmax​logp(θ∣D)=argmaxlogp(D∣θ)+logp(θ)

So θ^map=θ^mle\hat{\theta}_{map}=\hat{\theta}_{mle}θ^map​=θ^mle​ if the prior is uniform.

Another way to see the MLE is that the resulting predictive distribution p(y∣θ^mle)p(y|\hat{\theta}_{mle})p(y∣θ^mle​) is as close as possible to the empirical distribution of the data.

If we defined the empirical distribution by

pD(y)≜1N∑n=1Nδ(y−yn)\begin{equation} p_{\mathcal{D}}(\bold{y}) \triangleq \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N\delta(y-y_n) \end{equation}pD​(y)≜N1​n=1∑N​δ(y−yn​)​​

Minimizing the KL divergence between the empirical p=pDp=p_{\mathcal{D}}p=pD​ and an estimated distribution q=p(yn∣θ)q=p(y_n|\theta)q=p(yn​∣θ) is equivalent to minimizing the NLL and therefore computing the MLE.

DKL(p∣∣q)≜∑yp(y)log⁡p(y)q(y)=−H(pD)−1N∑n=1Nlog⁡p(yn∣θ)=const+NLL(θ)\begin{align} D_{\mathbb{KL}}(p||q) &\triangleq \sum_y p(y) \log \frac{p(y)}{q(y)} \\ &= -\mathbb{H}(p_{\mathcal{D}}) -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \log p(y_n|\theta) \\ &= \mathrm{const} +\mathrm{NLL}(\theta) \end{align} DKL​(p∣∣q)​≜y∑​p(y)logq(y)p(y)​=−H(pD​)−N1​n=1∑N​logp(yn​∣θ)=const+NLL(θ)​​

The same logic applies for supervised settings, with:

pD(x,y)=pD(y∣x)pD(x)=1N∑n=1Nδ(x−xn)δ(y−yn)p_{D}(x,y)=p_{\mathcal{D}}(y|x) p_{\mathcal{D}}(x)=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N \delta(x-x_n)\delta(y-y_n)pD​(x,y)=pD​(y∣x)pD​(x)=N1​n=1∑N​δ(x−xn​)δ(y−yn​)

4.2.3 MLE for the Bernoulli distribution

Let θ=p(Y=1)\theta =p(Y=1)θ=p(Y=1) be the probability of heads in a coin toss.

NLL(θ)=−log⁡∏n=1Np(yn∣θ)=−log⁡∏n=1NθI(yn=1)(1−θ)I(yn=0)=−[N1log⁡θ+N0log⁡(1−θ)]\begin{align} \mathrm{NLL}(\theta)&=-\log \prod^N_{n=1}p(y_n|\theta) \\ &=-\log \prod^N_{n=1}\theta^{\mathbb{I}(y_n=1)}(1-\theta)^{\mathbb{I}(y_n=0)} \\ &= -[N_1 \log\theta+N_0 \log(1-\theta)] \end{align}NLL(θ)​=−logn=1∏N​p(yn​∣θ)=−logn=1∏N​θI(yn​=1)(1−θ)I(yn​=0)=−[N1​logθ+N0​log(1−θ)]​​

The MLE can be found by:

ddθNLL(θ)=−N1θ+N01−θ⇒θ^mle=N1N\frac{d}{d\theta} \mathrm{NLL}(\theta)=- \frac{N_1}{\theta}+\frac{N_0}{1-\theta} \Rightarrow \hat{\theta}_{mle}=\frac{N_1}{N}dθd​NLL(θ)=−θN1​​+1−θN0​​⇒θ^mle​=NN1​​

4.2.4 MLE for the categorical distribution

Let Yn∼Cat(θ)Y_n \sim \mathrm{Cat}(\theta)Yn​∼Cat(θ).

NLL(θ)=−∑kNklog⁡θk\mathrm{NLL}(\theta)=-\sum_k N_k \log \theta_kNLL(θ)=−k∑​Nk​logθk​

To compute the MLE, we have to minimize the NLL subject the constraint ∑k=1Kθk=1\sum^K_{k=1}\theta_k=1∑k=1K​θk​=1 using the following Lagrangian:

L(θ,λ)≜−∑kNklog⁡θk−λ(1−∑kθk)\mathcal{L}(\theta,\lambda) \triangleq -\sum_kN_k \log \theta_k-\lambda \Big(1-\sum_k \theta_k\Big)L(θ,λ)≜−k∑​Nk​logθk​−λ(1−k∑​θk​)

We get the MLE by taking the derivative of L\mathcal{L}L for λ\lambdaλ and θk\theta_kθk​

θ^k=Nkλ=NkN\hat{\theta}_k=\frac{N_k}{\lambda}=\frac{N_k}{N}θ^k​=λNk​​=NNk​​

4.2.5 MLE for the univariate Gaussian

Suppose Y∼N(μ,σ2)Y \sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)Y∼N(μ,σ2). We estimate here again the parameters using the MLE:

NLL(μ,σ2)=−∑n=1Nlog⁡[(12πσ2)12exp⁡(−(yn−μ)22σ2)]=12σ2∑n=1N(yn−μ)2+N2log⁡(2πσ2)\begin{align} \mathrm{NLL}(\mu,\sigma^2) &=-\sum_{n=1}^N \log \Big[\big(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\big)^{\frac{1}{2}} \exp\big(-\frac{(y_n-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\big)\Big] \\ &= \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{n=1}^N(y_n-\mu)^2 + \frac{N}{2}\log (2\pi\sigma^2) \end{align}NLL(μ,σ2)​=−n=1∑N​log[(2πσ21​)21​exp(−2σ2(yn​−μ)2​)]=2σ21​n=1∑N​(yn​−μ)2+2N​log(2πσ2)​​

We find the stationary point by ∂∂μNLL(μ,σ2)=0\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu}\mathrm{NLL}(\mu,\sigma^2)=0∂μ∂​NLL(μ,σ2)=0 and ∂∂σ2NLL(μ,σ2)=0\frac{\partial}{\partial \sigma^2}\mathrm{NLL}(\mu,\sigma^2)=0∂σ2∂​NLL(μ,σ2)=0

μ^mle=1N∑n=1Nyn=yˉσ^mle2=1N∑n=1N(yn−μ^mle)2=s2−yˉ2s2≜1N∑n=1Nyn2\begin{align} \hat{\mu}_{mle} &=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N y_n=\bar{y} \\ \hat{\sigma}^2_{mle} &= \frac{1}{N}\sum^N_{n=1}(y_n-\hat{\mu}_{mle})^2=s^2- \bar{y}^2 \\ s^2& \triangleq \frac{1}{N}\sum^N_{n=1}y_n^2 \end{align} μ^​mle​σ^mle2​s2​=N1​n=1∑N​yn​=yˉ​=N1​n=1∑N​(yn​−μ^​mle​)2=s2−yˉ​2≜N1​n=1∑N​yn2​​​

yˉ\bar{y}yˉ​ and s2s^2s2 are the sufficient statistics of the data, since they are sufficient to compute the MLE.

The unbiased estimator for variance (not the MLE) is:

σ^unb2=1N−1∑n=1N(yn−μ^mle)2\hat{\sigma}^2_{unb}=\frac{1}{N-1} \sum^N_{n=1}(y_n-\hat{\mu}_{mle})^2σ^unb2​=N−11​n=1∑N​(yn​−μ^​mle​)2

4.2.6 MLE for MVN

LL(μ,Σ)=log⁡p(D∣μ,Σ)=−12∑n=1N(yn−μ)⊤Λ(yn−μ)+N2log⁡∣Λ∣\mathrm{LL}(\mu,\Sigma)=\log p(\mathcal{D}|\mu,\Sigma)=-\frac{1}{2}\sum_{n=1}^N(y_n-\mu)^\top\Lambda(y_n-\mu)+\frac{N}{2} \log|\Lambda|LL(μ,Σ)=logp(D∣μ,Σ)=−21​n=1∑N​(yn​−μ)⊤Λ(yn​−μ)+2N​log∣Λ∣

with Λ=Σ−1\Lambda=\Sigma^{-1}Λ=Σ−1 the precision matrix.

Using zn=yn−μz_n=y_n-\muzn​=yn​−μ:

∂∂μ(yn−μ)⊤Σ−1(yn−μ)=∂∂znzn⊤Σ−1zn∂zn∂μ=−(Σ−1+Σ−T)zn\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu}(y_n-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(y_n-\mu)=\frac{\partial}{\partial z_n}z_n^\top\Sigma^{-1}z_n\frac{\partial z_n}{\partial \mu}=-(\Sigma^{-1}+\Sigma^{-T})z_n∂μ∂​(yn​−μ)⊤Σ−1(yn​−μ)=∂zn​∂​zn⊤​Σ−1zn​∂μ∂zn​​=−(Σ−1+Σ−T)zn​

Hence

∂∂μLL(μ,Σ)=−12∑−2Σ−1(yn−μ)=0⇒μ^=yˉ\frac{\partial}{\partial \mu}\mathrm{LL}(\mu,\Sigma)=-\frac{1}{2}\sum -2\Sigma^{-1}(y_n-\mu)=0 \\\Rightarrow \hat{\mu}=\bar{y}∂μ∂​LL(μ,Σ)=−21​∑−2Σ−1(yn​−μ)=0⇒μ^​=yˉ​

So the MLE of μ\muμ is just the empirical mean.

Using the trace trick:

LL(μ^,Λ)=−12∑ntr[(yn−μ)(yn−μ)⊤Λ]+N2log⁡∣Λ∣=−12tr[SyˉΛ]+N2log⁡∣Λ∣\begin{align} \mathrm{LL}(\hat{\mu},\Lambda)&=-\frac{1}{2} \sum_ntr[(y_n-\mu)(y_n-\mu)^\top \Lambda]+\frac{N}{2}\log |\Lambda| \\ &= -\frac{1}{2} tr[S_{\bar{y}} \Lambda]+\frac{N}{2}\log |\Lambda| \end{align}LL(μ^​,Λ)​=−21​n∑​tr[(yn​−μ)(yn​−μ)⊤Λ]+2N​log∣Λ∣=−21​tr[Syˉ​​Λ]+2N​log∣Λ∣​​

With Syˉ=∑n(yn−yˉ)(yn−yˉ)⊤S_{\bar{y}}=\sum_n(y_n-\bar{y})(y_n-\bar{y})^\topSyˉ​​=∑n​(yn​−yˉ​)(yn​−yˉ​)⊤ the scatter matrix centered on yˉ\bar{y}yˉ​

Resolving the derivative for Λ\LambdaΛ gives Λ^−1=Σ=1NSyˉ\hat{\Lambda}^{-1}=\Sigma=\frac{1}{N} S_{\bar{y}}Λ^−1=Σ=N1​Syˉ​​

4.2.7 MLE for linear regression

Let suppose the model corresponds to p(y∣x,θ)=N(y∣w⊤x,σ2)p(y|x,\theta)=\mathcal{N}(y|w^\top x,\sigma^2)p(y∣x,θ)=N(y∣w⊤x,σ2). If we fix σ2\sigma^2σ2 to focus on www:

LL(w)=−∑n=1Nlog⁡[(12πσ2)1/2exp⁡(−12σ2(yn−w⊤xn)2)]\mathrm{LL}(w)=-\sum_{n=1}^N \log \Bigg[ \Big(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\Big)^{1/2} \exp\Big(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(y_n-w^\top x_n)^2\Big) \Bigg]LL(w)=−n=1∑N​log[(2πσ21​)1/2exp(−2σ21​(yn​−w⊤xn​)2)]

Dropping the irrelevant additive constants:

RSS(w)≜∑n=1N(yn−w⊤xn)2=∑n=1Nrn2\mathrm{RSS}(w) \triangleq \sum_{n=1}^N (y_n-w^\top x_n)^2=\sum^N_{n=1}r_n^2RSS(w)≜n=1∑N​(yn​−w⊤xn​)2=n=1∑N​rn2​

Note that MSE=1NRSS\mathrm{MSE}=\frac{1}{N}\mathrm{RSS}MSE=N1​RSS and RMSE=MSE\mathrm{RMSE}=\sqrt{MSE}RMSE=MSE​

Writing the RSS\mathrm{RSS}RSS in matrix notation:

RSS(w)=∣∣Xw⊤−y∣∣22\mathrm{RSS}(w)=||\bold{X}w^\top-\bold{y}||^2_2RSS(w)=∣∣Xw⊤−y∣∣22​

And the equation of OLS is:

w^mle≜arg min⁡wRSS(w)=(X⊤X)−1X⊤y\hat{w}_{mle} \triangleq \argmin_w \mathrm{RSS}(w) = \bold{(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y}w^mle​≜wargmin​RSS(w)=(X⊤X)−1X⊤y
