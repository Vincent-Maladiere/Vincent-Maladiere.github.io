11.4.2 Why does the ‚Ñì1\ell_1‚Ñì1‚ÄãÔªø yields sparse solutions?

The lasso objective is a non-smooth objective that can be expressed with the Lagrangian:
min‚Å°wNLL(w)s.t.‚à£‚à£w‚à£‚à£1‚â§B\min_{\bold{}w} \mathrm{NLL}(\bold{w})\quad s.t.\quad ||\bold{w}||_1\leq Bwmin‚ÄãNLL(w)s.t.‚à£‚à£w‚à£‚à£1‚Äã‚â§B
where BBBÔªø is an upper bound to the weight. A tight bound corresponds to a high Œª\lambdaŒªÔªø.
From the optimization theory, we know that the optimal solution lies at the intersection of the constraint surface and the lowest level of the objective function.
When we relax the constraint BBBÔªø, we grow the constraint surface until reaching the objective. The ‚Ñì1\ell_1‚Ñì1‚ÄãÔªø dice is more likely to hit the objective function on the corner rather than on the side, especially in high dimensions. The corners correspond to the sparse solution, lying on the coordinate axes.
Meanwhile, there are no corners on the ‚Ñì2\ell_2‚Ñì2‚ÄãÔªø ball, it can intersect the objective function everywhere; there is no preference for sparsity.

In standard ‚Ñì1\ell_1‚Ñì1‚ÄãÔªø regularization, we assume a 1:1 correspondence between a parameter and its variable. 11.4.8.1 Penalizing the two-norm
To encourage group sparsity, we partition the parameter vector into GGGÔªø groups, then we minimize:
PNNL(w)=NLL(w)+Œª‚àëg=1G‚à£‚à£wg‚à£‚à£2\mathrm{PNNL}(\bold{w})=\mathrm{NLL}(\bold{w})+\lambda \sum_{g=1}^G ||\bold{w}_g||_2PNNL(w)=NLL(w)+Œªg=1‚àëG‚Äã‚à£‚à£wg‚Äã‚à£‚à£2‚Äã
