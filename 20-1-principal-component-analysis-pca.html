20.1 Principal component analysis (PCA)

The simplest and most widely used form of dimensionality reduction is PCA. The basic idea is to find a linear and orthogonal projection of the high dimensional data x‚ààRD\bold{x}\in \R^Dx‚ààRDÔªø to a low dimensional subspace z‚ààRL\bold{z}\in \R^Lz‚ààRLÔªø, such that the low dimensional representation is a ‚Äúgood approximation‚Äù to the original data.

We define the following reconstruction error or distortion:
L(W)‚âú1N‚àën=1N‚à£‚à£xn‚àídecode(encode(xn;W);W))‚à£‚à£22\mathcal{L}(W)\triangleq \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N ||\bold{x}_n-\mathrm{decode}(\mathrm{encode(\bold{x}_n;W);W}))||_2^2L(W)‚âúN1‚Äãn=1‚àëN‚Äã‚à£‚à£xn‚Äã‚àídecode(encode(xn‚Äã;W);W))‚à£‚à£22‚Äã
where the encoder and decoder stages are both linear maps.
We can minimize this objective by setting W^=UL\hat{W}=U_LW^=UL‚ÄãÔªø, where ULU_LUL‚ÄãÔªø contains the LLLÔªø eigenvectors with the largest eigenvalues of the empirical covariance matrix:
